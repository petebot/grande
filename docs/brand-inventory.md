## Observed identity

### Logo and naming

- The available menu sheets do not show a sufficiently distinct, repeatable logo lockup.
- “Grande Burrito” appears as the business name in public listings, but the Freeport
  guide also uses the plural “Grande Burritos.” Canonical naming needs owner approval.
- No logo should be traced, reconstructed, or inferred from a social thumbnail.
- Until an owner-supplied mark is licensed, the site should use an accessible text name
  rather than present a new drawing as the existing logo.

### Typography

Observed:

- Heavy, high-impact serif type carries item and section names.
- Supporting text is also serif in the photographed menus, generally regular weight.
- Scale changes—more than decorative rules or boxes—create most of the hierarchy.
- Centered composition gives the sheets an informal, homemade menu character.

Not established:

- The exact font family, weight files, source, or license.
- Whether the menu typography is a deliberate long-term brand choice or a document-tool
  default.

The current development site’s Georgia/system-serif stack is therefore a provisional
stand-in, not a claimed match.

### Palette

Observed with high confidence:

- Near-black ink on white or warm-white paper is the dominant system.
- Color appears sparingly through emoji: red hearts, blue bowls, yellow lemons, and food
  pictograms.

Not established:

- No photographed color is reliable enough to define an exact production hex value.
- The current site’s deep green, brick red, cream, and paper tones are proposed digital
  extensions. They are not documented as existing brand colors.

### Layout and graphic cues

- One direct reading column with strong vertical rhythm.
- Item names act as display headlines; ingredients read as compact follow-up lines.
- Ingredients are joined with plus signs, producing an energetic “built from parts”
  texture.
- Prices sit inline with descriptions instead of in a separate accounting column.
- Large all-caps category moments interrupt the menu for kids, beverages, add-ons, and
  specials.
- Emoji work as informal dividers and emphasis marks rather than as essential meaning.
- The physical menu’s border/lamination suggests a contained “menu sheet” surface.

### Voice

The supplied menus are direct, generous, and lightly playful. They use conversational
prompts, emphatic punctuation, emoji, and plain ingredient lists rather than polished
fine-dining language. Public directory descriptions also emphasize freshness, fun,
large burritos, and casual service. These are tone cues only; marketing claims and exact
phrasing still require owner approval.

### Storefront and place

- Confirmed public context places the business at 115 Main Street in downtown Freeport.
- The available evidence does not include a project-owned storefront photograph or a
  documented interior-material palette.
- Historic-building context may inform restraint and hospitality, but the website must
  not invent colonial motifs or claim architectural heritage as restaurant branding.

### Imagery

- No food, team, interior, or storefront photograph is currently cleared for website use.
- User-supplied menu photos are research evidence only.
- Social or directory images must not be copied without direct permission or a documented
  license.
- The public experience must remain useful when every optional image is absent.

## Decisions and non-decisions

### Safe to explore now

- Paper, ink, serif hierarchy, compact ingredient copy, sparse playful accents, and
  menu-like rules.
- Responsive layouts that prioritize current status, menu, call, and directions.
- A future print view generated from the same menu data as the website.
- Image-optional composition and decorative motifs with empty alternative text.

### Hold for owner confirmation

- Canonical singular/plural business name and any tagline.
- Logo files, clear-space rules, and allowed variants.
- Exact fonts and brand colors.
- Current menu, prices, hours, dietary claims, alcohol details, and social accounts.
- Rights for all photography and any custom illustration.

### Explicitly reject

- A fabricated “Mexican restaurant” visual vocabulary not present in the evidence.
- Tracing a low-resolution social logo or treating emoji as the logo.
- Republishing menu photos as the public menu.
- Maintaining separate web and print menu facts.
- Using color, motion, imagery, or hover as the only carrier of meaning.

## Owner-review questions

1. Is the canonical public name “Grande Burrito” or “Grande Burritos”?
2. Can the owner supply original logo files and identify who created them?
3. Were the menu fonts and emoji chosen intentionally, and should they continue online?
4. Which colors, signs, interior materials, or packaging feel most recognizably theirs?
5. Which social accounts are official and who controls recovery access?
6. May the project use supplied food, storefront, team, and interior photography, and
   where is permission recorded?
7. Should a print-ready menu generated from Sanity replace the current document workflow?
